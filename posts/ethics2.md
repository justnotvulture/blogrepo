*The Irreducible Weight of Suffering*

**The Irreducible Weight of Suffering:**

Ethics, Fear, and the Foundations of Moral Action

*On Why We Should Do Good in a World That May or May Not Be Watching*


# **Introduction: The Oldest Question, Restated**
There is a question so old that it has accumulated centuries of dust without losing any of its urgency: Why should I be good?

This is not a naïve question. It is, in fact, one of the most philosophically demanding questions a person can ask, because it cuts beneath every surface-level account of morality and forces us to examine the bedrock. When Dostoevsky's Ivan Karamazov declared that "if God does not exist, everything is permitted," he was not being glib. He was articulating a genuine anxiety: that without a transcendent legislator, moral obligation dissolves into mere preference, and ethics becomes indistinguishable from aesthetics or appetite. This anxiety has never fully disappeared from Western moral thought, and it deserves to be taken seriously, not dismissed.

Yet the question can be reframed. The more philosophically interesting formulation is not "why be good if there is no God?" but rather: what kind of goodness are we actually after? And here a troubling possibility emerges — one that has been noticed by moral psychologists, virtue ethicists, and theologians alike. It is possible to perform all the right actions — to give to the poor, to abstain from cruelty, to honor one's obligations — while doing so from a motivation so fundamentally corrupted that the goodness in question is, at some deep level, hollow. An agent who helps a drowning child because she fears divine punishment is, by most accounts, doing something less morally admirable than one who helps because she recognizes the reality and urgency of the child's suffering. Both have saved a life. But they have not performed the same moral act.

This essay takes that distinction seriously. It argues, across several layers of analysis, that moral action grounded in the direct recognition of suffering — its reality, its weight, its claim on us — is both philosophically more defensible and psychologically more generative than moral action grounded primarily in fear of cosmic punishment or hope for transcendent reward. This is not an argument against religion as such. Billions of human beings hold religious beliefs of enormous depth and seriousness, and many of them have been driven by those beliefs to acts of extraordinary moral courage.

The argument here is more specific and more careful: it is an argument against **fear-based moral motivation** as a foundation, and against the social and psychological pathologies that emerge when certain forms of rigid, authoritarian religiosity come to dominate the public moral imagination of a society.

The United States provides a particularly instructive case study — not because American religion is uniquely dangerous, but because the country has developed, in certain quarters, what we might call a superreligious tendency — a mode of religious life in which theological certainty, political ambition, and moral absolutism fuse into something that is less about inner transformation and more about enforcing conformity. Understanding this tendency, and distinguishing it carefully from ordinary faith and private spirituality, is part of the work this essay undertakes.

# **I. Philosophical Foundations of Morality Without Religion**
The philosophical tradition contains at least four major frameworks for grounding moral obligation without recourse to divine command, and each of them illuminates something important about the nature of the question.
## **Consequentialism**
**Consequentialism**, most associated with Bentham and Mill, holds that the morally right action is the one that produces the greatest well-being for the greatest number of affected parties. The genius of consequentialism as a secular moral framework is its refusal to look away from the physical and psychological reality of experience. Suffering hurts. Pleasure is preferable to pain. These are not metaphysical speculations; they are observable, verifiable facts about sentient life.

If we accept that the reduction of unnecessary suffering is a good worth pursuing, then we have a moral framework that requires no supernatural backing. The suffering of a starving child is a morally relevant fact about the world regardless of whether God exists, regardless of whether the child will be compensated in an afterlife. The suffering is here, and it is real, and its claim on our attention does not wait for theological resolution.

Consequentialism has well-known vulnerabilities — it can, under certain formulations, justify serious injustices to individuals for the sake of aggregate benefit — but its core intuition is morally serious and difficult to dismiss. The tangible, observable reality of suffering provides a foundation that is, in an important sense, more epistemically secure than claims about divine will. We can argue endlessly about what God wants. We cannot argue that a person in agony is not in agony.
## **Virtue Ethics**
**Virtue ethics**, the tradition descending from Aristotle, approaches the question differently. Rather than asking what actions maximize good outcomes, it asks what kind of person one ought to be. The virtuous person — courageous, just, temperate, practically wise — does good not because she calculates outcomes and not because she fears punishment, but because she has cultivated a character from which good action flows naturally.

Virtue ethics shifts the moral center of gravity from external enforcement to internal formation. The question it poses is not "what rules must I follow?" but "what must I become?" This shift is philosophically significant, because it suggests that moral action divorced from genuine character development is, at best, incomplete. A person who gives to charity only when observed by God or community is not virtuous in the Aristotelian sense; she is merely compliant.

For Aristotle, the good life — eudaimonia, often translated as flourishing or happiness — is inseparable from the exercise of virtue. This is not a life lived under threat or in pursuit of cosmic reward. It is a life in which moral excellence is understood as constitutive of genuine human flourishing, not merely instrumental to some further end.
## **Kantian Deontology**
**Kantian deontology** offers a third path. For Kant, the moral law is derived not from God and not from consequences but from the structure of rational agency itself. The categorical imperative — act only according to that maxim by which you can at the same time will that it become a universal law — is binding on all rational creatures precisely because it is a deliverable of reason, not of revelation.

Kant was himself a deeply religious man, but he was careful to insist that the moral law is not grounded in divine command; rather, the existence of the moral law gives us a rational basis for certain religious beliefs. This inversion is philosophically decisive. It means that moral obligation precedes and grounds theology, rather than the reverse.
## **Existentialist Ethics**
**Existentialist ethics**, finally, approaches the question from a radically different direction. For Sartre and Camus, there is no pre-given moral order, no God to supply meaning, no essence that precedes existence. But this is not, for them, a counsel of despair or nihilism. It is an invitation to radical responsibility.

Camus, in particular, argued that the appropriate human response to an indifferent universe is not resignation but rebellion — a determined insistence on human solidarity, on reducing unnecessary suffering, on refusing the consolations of easy transcendence. His ethics is perhaps the most nakedly honest about the difficulty of moral commitment in a world without guarantees: we are good not because we will be rewarded, but because suffering is real and human solidarity is a choice worth making.
## **The Foundation of Empathy**
These four frameworks disagree about much. But they share a crucial feature: they ground moral obligation in features of human experience — suffering, reason, character, solidarity — that are available to any reflective person regardless of metaphysical commitments.

**Empathy**, which developmental psychologists like Martin Hoffman have studied extensively, appears to be a natural endowment of social animals. Human beings, under normal developmental conditions, feel distress when they witness others suffering. This is not a cultural imposition; it is a feature of our species' biology, shaped by millions of years of social living. The capacity to recognize another's pain as pain — to see in the face of a suffering stranger something that makes a claim on you — is not dependent on religious instruction. It is, if anything, prior to it.

Religious traditions have often deepened and extended this natural capacity, channeling it through narrative, ritual, and community. But they did not create it, and their absence does not destroy it. The question is not whether morality can exist without religion — clearly it can and does — but whether religion provides additional moral motivation or structure that secular frameworks cannot supply.

# **II. Corrupted Motivation and the Moral Psychology of Fear**
The concept of moral motivation — why one does what one does — has a complex relationship to moral evaluation. Most ordinary moral thinking focuses on the act: did you help or harm? did you lie or tell the truth? But a richer moral psychology attends to the springs of action as well, and here a significant distinction opens up.

The psychologists Edward Deci and Richard Ryan, in their work on self-determination theory, distinguished between intrinsic and extrinsic motivation. Intrinsically motivated behavior is undertaken for its own sake — because it is meaningful, satisfying, or because one genuinely cares about the outcome. Extrinsically motivated behavior is undertaken as a means to some external end — praise, reward, avoidance of punishment. Their research consistently showed that extrinsic motivation tends to crowd out intrinsic motivation over time.

This has direct implications for moral psychology. A person who refrains from cruelty because she has internalized a genuine concern for others — because she feels the wrongness of cruelty in a visceral, motivationally operative way — is in a very different psychological condition than a person who refrains from cruelty because she fears divine punishment. The first person's moral behavior is likely to be consistent across contexts, including contexts in which no one is watching and punishment is not possible. The second person's moral behavior is contingent on her belief in divine surveillance.

Studies in moral development have consistently shown that children raised with primarily authoritarian, punishment-based moral education tend to develop what Lawrence Kohlberg called preconventional or conventional moral reasoning — compliance with rules to avoid punishment or to maintain social approval — rather than postconventional reasoning, which involves the internalization of genuine principles that guide behavior independently of external enforcement.

The moral philosopher Immanuel Kant had a version of this worry. He distinguished between actions done from duty and actions done in conformity with duty. A person who tells the truth because she fears the consequences of being caught in a lie acts in conformity with duty — the right thing happens — but not from duty. For Kant, only the latter kind of action has genuine moral worth.
## **The Case for Fear-Based Morality**
The defender of fear-based religious morality can raise a legitimate objection. She might argue that the destination matters more than the journey — that a society in which people refrain from murder, theft, and cruelty because they fear God is better than one in which such restraints are absent, whatever the motivational quality of the restraint. This is not an absurd argument. In conditions of severe social instability or moral chaos, fear-based moral systems may serve as a stabilizing force that more sophisticated ethical systems cannot yet replace.

This concession is important. It would be intellectually dishonest to deny that religion has historically played an organizing and stabilizing role in moral life. The critique of fear-based motivation is not a critique of religion per se; it is a critique of a particular mode of moral reasoning that elevates cosmic fear above the direct recognition of human suffering and dignity.

Many religious traditions have themselves mounted precisely this critique. The Jewish prophetic tradition consistently attacked ritual compliance as inferior to genuine justice. Jesus' teaching, in the Sermon on the Mount and elsewhere, focused on the transformation of inner disposition rather than external conformity. The Sufi tradition in Islam emphasizes the love of God and humanity over legalistic observance. The critique of corrupted motivation is, in many respects, a deeply religious critique that secular philosophy has inherited and formalized.

The deeper problem with fear-based moral motivation is structural. A moral system grounded primarily in fear requires a credible threat. If people begin to doubt the reality of divine punishment — as has happened at increasing rates in modernizing societies — the moral restraints that depend on that fear can dissolve rapidly, and nothing in the fear-based system itself provides an alternative foundation. Compassion-based morality, by contrast, does not depend on the continued credibility of a threat; it depends on the continued salience of human suffering, which is not going anywhere.

# **III. Superreligionism and Its Pathologies**
The term "superreligionism" is not standard in academic literature, but it captures something real: a mode of religious life in which theological conviction becomes so total, so demanding, and so politically ambitious that it functions less as a spiritual orientation and more as a comprehensive identity system that organizes all of social reality into a framework of absolute certainty.

It is important to be precise about what this does and does not describe. Most religious believers in the United States and elsewhere are not superreligionists. They hold their faith with varying degrees of intensity, they navigate the tensions between their religious commitments and the demands of a pluralistic society with varying degrees of grace, and their religion functions primarily as a source of personal meaning, community, and ethical orientation rather than as a mandate for political dominance.

The Catholic grandmother who prays the rosary every morning and volunteers at the local food pantry is not the subject of this analysis. Neither is the evangelical pastor who runs a homeless shelter and preaches a message of personal transformation without claiming the right to impose that transformation on others by force.
## **Defining Features of Superreligionism**
Superreligionism describes something more specific: a configuration of religious belief in which the certainty of divine truth authorizes the subordination of pluralism, democratic deliberation, and the rights of dissenters to the demands of a theological agenda. Its distinguishing features are not piety or theological seriousness — both of which can coexist with genuine respect for others — but a combination of three elements:

**Epistemic closure** — the conviction that one's own reading of divine will is not merely probably correct but certainly correct. **Boundary maximalism** — the insistence on a sharp and morally loaded distinction between the in-group of true believers and the out-group of the spiritually or morally defective. And **coercive aspiration** — the desire to use social, legal, or political power to enforce conformity with theological norms.
## **The Sociology of In-Group Dynamics**
The sociological literature on in-group/out-group dynamics is extensive and well-established. Henri Tajfel's social identity theory demonstrated that even minimal group membership produces psychological effects: elevation of the in-group, derogation of the out-group, and the perception of homogeneity and threat in those outside one's group. Religious identity, because it is so deeply bound up with questions of ultimate meaning and self-worth, can intensify these dynamics significantly.

When theological certainty is added — when one believes that one's group has direct access to absolute truth while out-group members are either deceived or wicked — the psychological pressure toward out-group hostility increases substantially. The moral psychologist Jonathan Haidt's work on moral foundations theory reveals why superreligionism is particularly dangerous in moral-political contexts: the "purity" intuition, when activated in a religious register, can generate a moral phenomenology of absolute disgust and revulsion at out-group practices that makes compromise feel not merely undesirable but morally impossible.
## **Historical and Contemporary Manifestations**
This dynamic has historical precedents of the gravest kind. The religious wars of early modern Europe, the organized persecution of minority communities in the name of Christian civilization, the various campaigns of forced conversion carried out by imperial powers claiming divine mandate — these were not aberrations produced by religion's worst fringe. They were, in many cases, the organized expression of what were, for their time, mainstream theological convictions.

This is not an argument that religion causes violence; the historical record is too complex for such a simple claim. It is an argument that the combination of absolute theological certainty, political ambition, and sharp in-group/out-group thinking has, under a range of historical conditions, produced serious moral catastrophe.

In the contemporary United States, superreligionism takes forms that are less obviously violent but philosophically troubling in their own right: the erosion of epistemic norms in public discourse, the reduction of political questions to moral absolutes that admit of no compromise, the treatment of democratic opponents not as fellow citizens with different but legitimate values but as agents of spiritual evil, and the deployment of religious authority to suppress the kind of moral learning and revision that any serious ethics requires.

One of the most philosophically interesting features of superreligious politics is its relationship to moral complexity. Genuine ethical reasoning — whether secular or religious — involves the recognition that most important moral questions are genuinely difficult, that competing values must be weighed against one another, that reasonable people can reach different conclusions while both reasoning carefully. Superreligionism tends to dissolve this complexity into certainty. If God has clearly spoken, then difficult policy questions are not questions requiring difficult deliberation; they have answers already determined by divine decree, which only the wicked or stupid could fail to see.

It is worth noting, with philosophical fairness, that superreligionism is not the only form this pathology takes. Secular ideological movements have produced their own versions of epistemic closure and coercive aspiration — the totalitarian movements of the twentieth century being the most extreme examples. The critique here is not that religion is uniquely pathological but that the particular combination of divine authority claims and political ambition creates a distinctive and difficult-to-contest form of moral authoritarianism.
